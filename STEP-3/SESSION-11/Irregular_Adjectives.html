Irregular Adjectives

Mastering Exceptions in English

While most adjectives follow standard rules, a select group completely transforms. Memorizing these irregular forms is crucial for advanced fluency.

1

The Transformation Matrix

Base Form Comparative Superlative
good better (the) best
bad worse (the) worst
far farther / further (the) farthest / furthest
little (amount) less (the) least
many / much more (the) most
old older / elder* (the) oldest / eldest*
late later / latter* (the) latest / last*
near nearer (the) nearest / next*
well (adverb) better (the) best
badly (adverb) worse (the) worst
2

Crucial Distinctions

Elder / Eldest

These forms are used strictly for people, usually within a family, not for objects or buildings.

"He is my elder brother."

Latter / Last

They refer specifically to order or time, indicating the second of two things mentioned, or the final item.

"The latter part of the movie was great."

Next vs Nearest

They differ in meaning. Nearest is about physical distance, while Next is about sequence or order.

"Nearest station" (Distance) "Next station" (Sequence)

Adverbs

Adverbs like well and badly follow the exact same irregular pattern as their adjective counterparts (good/bad).

well ➔ better ➔ best badly ➔ worse ➔ worst
