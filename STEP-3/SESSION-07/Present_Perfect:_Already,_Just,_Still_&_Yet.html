Already, Just, Still & Yet

Master the adverbs of time in the Present Perfect to express completion, recency, and continuation.

Just

Used for recently finished actions. It adds the sense of "a moment ago".

Position

Between have/has and the past participle form of the verb.

- Dad's just washed up.
(He finished a few minutes ago.)

Already

Shows that the action is complete. It happened sooner than expected.

Position

Between have/has and the past participle form of the verb.

- Dad's already washed up.
(It's done, no matter when.)

Still

Used in negative sentences to mean "the situation continues".

Position

Before the negative haven't / hasn't.

- Dad still hasn't washed up.
(Plates are still dirty.)

? Yet

Appears in negative sentences or questions, meaning "until now".

Position

At the very end of the sentence.

- Dad hasn't washed up yet.

- Has Dad washed up yet?

The Golden Rule

Always connect Just with "time freshness" (a moment ago), Already with "completion" (done), and Yet / Still with "waiting" for an action to happen.
