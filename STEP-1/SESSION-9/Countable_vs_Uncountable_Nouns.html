Countable vs. Uncountable Nouns

Nouns in English behave differently depending on whether you can count them as individual items or not.

Countable Nouns

Things you can count one by one.

They can be singular or plural, and we can use numbers or articles like a/an.

  • • I have two dogs.
  • • Sandra has three cars.
  • • Jessie has ten dollars.

Use with:

a/an, one, two, many, few, several

Uncountable Nouns

Things you can't count individually.

Often abstract ideas, liquids, materials, or mass nouns. They are always singular and never used with a/an.

  • • Mack drinks a lot of water.
  • • Cindy gives great advice.
  • • Paul enjoys politics. (Ends in "s", but uncountable!)

Use with:

some, much, a lot of, a little

Common Mistakes

✕ I need an advice.
→ ✓ I need some advice.
✕ She gave me many money.
→ ✓ She gave me a lot of money.
✕ There are informations.
→ ✓ There is some information.

Tips to Remember

•

Some uncountable nouns look plural (e.g., news, politics, mathematics), but they're singular!

•

Use containers to count uncountables:

  • ✓ a glass of water
  • ✓ a piece of advice
  • ✓ a bottle of oil
