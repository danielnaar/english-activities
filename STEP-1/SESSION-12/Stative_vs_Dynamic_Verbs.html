Stative vs. Dynamic Verbs

Understand the difference between verbs of action and verbs of state to master continuous tenses in English.

What Are They?

Dynamic Verbs

They describe actions or events that start and finish. You can usually see them happening.

e.g. run, eat, write

✓

They can be used in continuous tenses (is running, was writing).

Stative Verbs

They describe states, feelings, thoughts, possession, and conditions that don't change quickly.

✕

They are usually not used in continuous tenses.
("I am knowing" is wrong).

Comparison Table

Feature Dynamic Verb Stative Verb
Meaning Action, change, process Emotion, belief, possession, perception
Example "She is playing tennis." "She knows the truth."
Tense Use Usually OK in continuous tenses Not used in continuous forms

Examples to Practice

Dynamic

play run eat write think (considering) smell (sniff)

Stative

know love hate believe own seem need prefer

Dynamic vs Stative use:

"I have a car."

Stative

"I'm having dinner at 7pm."

Dynamic

Teaching Tip

Ask students, "Can you film this action?"

If yes, it's likely Dynamic.
If it's internal (feel, know, own), it's Stative.

Warning

Some verbs (think, smell, have, taste) can act both ways.

Always consider context!
