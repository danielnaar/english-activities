Use of CAN

Fast map, clear forms, and real examples to speak naturally.

1) Quick Meaning Map

Ability (now)

I can swim.

Permission

Can I sit here?

Requests / Offers

Can you help me?

Possibility (general)

It can be cold in April.

Senses

I can hear music.

Deduction (negative)

You can't be hungry.

2) Core Forms

Affirmative

Subject + can + base verb

She can play guitar.

Negative

Subject + cannot/can't + base verb

They can't come today.

Note: cannot = can't

Question

Can + subject + base verb + ?

Can you open the window?

Use can for general or present ability. For specific ability in the past or difficult situations, you will often see be able to.

Short answers Yes, I can. / No, I can't.

Strict Rules

  • ✓ No "to" after can: can to
  • ✓ No "-s" on can: She cans

3 Everyday Examples

Ability

I can speak French. She can't drive.

Permission

Can I use your phone? You can sit here. Sorry, you can't come in.

Requests & offers

Can you give me a hand? Can I help?

General possibility

It can be windy in March. The trip can take 40 minutes.

With the senses

I can see the mountains. Can you hear that noise? I can't smell anything.

4 Deduction with can't

"You can't be hungry. You have just eaten."

"He can't be at home. I saw him in London an hour ago."

Opposite Idea

Use must for a strong logical guess in the affirmative.

Example: It is dark outside. They must be asleep.

5 Pronunciation Guide

can often sounds like kən in sentences:
"I kən wait."

can't is strong: kænt.
Contrast: I can go vs. I can't go.

6 Common Mistakes

✕ She cans swim.
→ ✓ She can swim.
✕ Can you to help me?
→ ✓ Can you help me?
✕ You can to use my phone.
→ ✓ You can use my phone.

Tip: keep the base verb after can/can't (no to, no -ing).
